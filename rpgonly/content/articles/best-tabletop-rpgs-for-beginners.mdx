D&D is the gateway drug. But once you've played a campaign or two, you start wondering: *what else is out there?*

The answer is: a lot. The tabletop RPG hobby is enormous, with hundreds of games covering every conceivable genre, tone, and playstyle. The problem is knowing where to start.

This guide covers five of the best beginner-accessible TTRPGs that aren't D&D — games with clear rules, active communities, great starter products, and enough depth to keep you playing for years.

## What Makes a Good Beginner TTRPG?

We judged each game on:

- **Rules clarity** — Can a new GM run this without a PhD in rulebooks?
- **Starter product quality** — Is there a free or cheap intro product?
- **Community size** — Are there people to play with and resources to learn from?
- **Tone variety** — Does it offer something different from D&D?
- **Cost to start** — Cheaper to start = better for beginners

## 1. Pathfinder 2nd Edition — The D&D Alternative

If you love D&D but want more — more character customization, more tactical depth, more content — Pathfinder 2e is the obvious choice.

Published by Paizo, PF2e uses a similar-but-better action economy (three actions per turn, each spent on move/attack/spell/anything), has dozens of classes with granular feat customization, and has a staggering library of content.

The base rules are also **completely free** via the Archives of Nethys website, which is a massive win.

<Callout type="tip" title="Free Quickstart">
Paizo publishes the Beginner Box — a self-contained starter product with simplified rules, a pre-made adventure, and character sheets. It's the best RPG starter kit on the market.
</Callout>

**What it does better than D&D:** Character customization depth, tactical combat, rule consistency, free legal rules access.

**What it does worse:** The sheer volume of content is overwhelming for brand new GMs. Start with the Beginner Box.

## 2. Call of Cthulhu 7th Edition — Horror & Investigation

Want to play something completely different from fantasy dungeon-crawling? Call of Cthulhu (CoC) is a horror-investigation game set in the 1920s, based on H.P. Lovecraft's Cthulhu Mythos.

Instead of heroes, you play **investigators** — ordinary people thrust into confrontations with cosmic horrors too powerful to fight. Success means surviving with your sanity intact (sometimes). Failure means death, madness, or worse.

It's mechanically approachable (percentile dice system, simple skills), narratively rich, and offers an experience no fantasy game can replicate.

<Callout type="warning" title="Content Warning">
Call of Cthulhu deals with themes of madness, cosmic horror, and hopelessness. Some published adventures also contain historical racism in their depictions of the 1920s era. Choose published scenarios thoughtfully and discuss expectations with your group.
</Callout>

**Best for:** Mystery/horror fans, groups who want roleplay-heavy sessions, people who are tired of fighting things with swords.

**Free starter:** The Chaosium website offers a free Quick-Start rules PDF.

## 3. Ironsworn — The Solo/Cooperative RPG

Ironsworn is a revelation. It's a dark fantasy RPG designed to be played **solo** (just you), **cooperatively** (group with no GM), or traditionally (GM + players). All three modes work beautifully.

The system uses d6s and a d10 oracle table. When you attempt something risky, you roll two dice and try to beat a value. The system generates emergent storytelling through its oracle tables — you ask questions, roll dice, and interpret the results to create your story.

The full Ironsworn core rulebook is **free** (PDF). The follow-up, Starforged (sci-fi setting), is $20.

<Callout type="lore" title="Why Ironsworn Matters">
Most RPGs require multiple people to schedule and a GM to prep extensively. Ironsworn breaks both barriers. You can run a deep, satisfying campaign solo in 30-minute sessions while waiting for your friends' schedules to align.
</Callout>

**Best for:** Solo players, groups without a dedicated GM, fans of dark Norse-inspired fantasy.

**Cost to start:** Free.

## 4. Blades in the Dark — Heist & Crime

Blades in the Dark (BitD) is the best heist game ever made. You play a crew of scoundrels in a dark, industrial fantasy city — thieves, assassins, cult enforcers, and spirit traffickers pulling jobs to climb the underworld ladder.

The design innovation is that you don't plan before the job — you **flashback** during it. Instead of "okay, we need to get into the vault — let's plan this out," you simply say "I've already obtained the guard's keycard" and flash back to show how you got it, then roll. This eliminates boring planning sessions and keeps the pace snappy.

**Best for:** Groups who love heist movies, moral grey areas, and narrative-first play.

**Access:** The complete rules are freely available online via Creative Commons at bladesinthedark.com.

## 5. Monster of the Week — Action Horror TV-Style

Based on the Powered by the Apocalypse system, Monster of the Week (MotW) lets you play a team of monster hunters — think Supernatural, Buffy the Vampire Slayer, or The X-Files. Each week you investigate a new monster, gather clues, and take it down.

The system is built for episodic play, which means sessions are self-contained and much easier for busy groups to schedule. The GM prep is lighter too — the system gives GMs tools to generate monsters and threats on the fly.

**Best for:** TV genre fans, groups who can't commit to a long campaign, new GMs who are intimidated by big prep.

## Quick Comparison

| Game | Tone | Complexity | Best For |
|------|------|-----------|----------|
| Pathfinder 2e | Epic Fantasy | Medium | D&D fans wanting more depth |
| Call of Cthulhu | Horror | Low-Medium | Mystery/horror lovers |
| Ironsworn | Dark Fantasy | Low | Solo or GM-less play |
| Blades in the Dark | Crime | Medium | Heist/narrative fans |
| Monster of the Week | Action Horror | Low | Casual groups, new GMs |

## The Verdict

**If you want a direct D&D upgrade:** Pathfinder 2e.

**If you want something completely different:** Call of Cthulhu or Blades in the Dark.

**If scheduling is hard:** Ironsworn (solo) or Monster of the Week (episodic).

The beauty of the tabletop RPG hobby is that no single game can be everything to everyone. Try a few. Most have free quickstart rules. Some of your best gaming memories might come from a game you'd never heard of six months ago.
